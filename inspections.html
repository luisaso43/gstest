Inspection Services

Choose the Right Inspection

Every property and every insurance policy is different — here's what each inspection covers.

For Insurance Renewal

4-Point Inspection

A focused review of a home's four major systems, commonly required by insurance companies for homes over a certain age.

  • Roof — age, condition, and remaining life
  • Electrical — panel, wiring, and safety concerns
  • Plumbing — pipe materials and visible leaks
  • HVAC — condition and functionality
For Insurance Discounts

Wind Mitigation Inspection

Documents your home's storm-resistant features, which can help lower windstorm insurance premiums.

  • Roof covering, shape, and deck attachment
  • Roof-to-wall connections
  • Opening protection (windows, doors, garage)
  • Secondary water resistance barrier
For Buyers & Sellers

Full Residential Inspection

A comprehensive, top-to-bottom evaluation of a home's condition — ideal before buying, selling, or listing.

  • Structure, foundation, and roof
  • Electrical, plumbing, and HVAC systems
  • Interior, exterior, attic, and crawlspace
  • Detailed report with photos and findings
The Basics

What Is a Home Inspection?

A home inspection is a visual, non-invasive evaluation of a property's condition, performed by a trained inspector who examines the systems and structure a homeowner relies on every day.

The goal isn't to pass or fail a house — it's to give you an honest, documented picture of its current condition, so you can make an informed decision with confidence, whether you're buying, selling, or simply maintaining your home.

You'll receive a detailed report with photos, notes, and clear explanations of anything that needs attention, organized so it's easy to understand and easy to act on.

What Is Inspected?

Before The Inspector Arrives

Preparing Your Home for Inspection

A little preparation helps the inspection go faster and gives a clearer picture of your home's condition.

  • Turn on all utilities — electricity, gas, and water
  • Provide clear access to the attic, electrical panel, and water heater
  • Unlock all doors, gates, and outbuildings
  • Replace burnt-out lightbulbs so fixtures can be tested
  • Clear items stored around HVAC units and water shutoffs
  • Secure pets away from the inspection areas
  • Have prior repair records or permits on hand, if available
  • Ensure the inspector will have working keys or codes for any locks

Closing The Deal

Before Closing

The inspection report often becomes part of the negotiation — here's what to keep in mind.

  • Review repair requests or credits with your real estate agent
  • Request a re-inspection for any major repairs completed by the seller
  • Do a final walkthrough to confirm the home's condition hasn't changed
  • Confirm agreed-upon repairs were completed as promised
  • Keep a copy of the inspection report for your records
After Closing

After Closing

Your inspection report is a useful reference well beyond move-in day.

  • Keep the report on file for warranty and insurance purposes
  • Budget for any items noted as "near end of life"
  • Schedule routine maintenance for HVAC, roof, and plumbing systems
  • Know the location of your main water and electrical shutoffs
Settling In

Move-In Checklist

A few simple steps to make your first weeks in a new home easier and safer.

  • Change or re-key the exterior locks
  • Test all smoke and carbon monoxide detectors
  • Locate the main water shutoff valve
  • Locate and label the electrical breaker panel
  • Set up or transfer utilities and internet service
  • Replace HVAC filters and note the system's maintenance schedule
  • Deep clean before unpacking, especially kitchen and bathrooms
  • Update your address with the post office, bank, and providers
  • Review any home warranties or appliance manuals left behind
  • Introduce yourself to neighbors and learn trash/recycling days
